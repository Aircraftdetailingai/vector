Why Automotive Detailing Apps Don't Work for Aircraft

Every month, someone in an aviation detailing Facebook group asks: "Can I just use [insert automotive detailing app] for my aircraft work?" The answer is always the same: you can try, but you'll outgrow it on your first large cabin job.

I know because I tried. I spent two years forcing Urable, then Jobber, then a custom Airtable setup to handle aircraft detailing workflows. None of them worked. Not because they're bad products — they're excellent at what they do. They just weren't built for what we do.

The Pricing Model Is Fundamentally Different

Automotive detailing apps price by vehicle size category. Small sedan, midsize SUV, large truck. Maybe 4-6 tiers. You pick the tier, the price is set, done.

Aircraft detailing doesn't work that way. There's no "midsize jet" tier that covers both a Citation CJ3 and a Challenger 350. The CJ3 has roughly 1,950 square feet of painted surface. The Challenger 350 has roughly 3,600. That's nearly double the surface area — and nearly double the labor hours for any paint service.

We need to price by specific aircraft model with hours calculated from actual surface area. A Gulfstream G4 takes 76 hours to polish. A G550 takes 95 hours. A G650 takes 110 hours. These aren't rounding differences — they're the difference between a profitable job and working for free.

No automotive app has a database of 300+ aircraft models with surface-area-based labor hour calculations. That's not a feature they forgot — it's a feature their market doesn't need.

The Quote Process Is Different

In automotive detailing, most quotes happen in person. The customer drives up, you look at the car, you give a price. Maybe you send a quick text confirmation.

In aircraft detailing, the aircraft might be 1,000 miles away. The customer might be a fleet manager who's never seen the aircraft in person. You're quoting based on a tail number, a make/model, and maybe a few photos. The quote needs to be detailed, professional, and include enough information for the decision-maker to approve it without seeing you or the aircraft.

That means your quoting tool needs FAA tail number lookup, aircraft-specific hour calculations, and a professional customer-facing quote page — not a text message with a number.

The Customer Relationship Is Different

Automotive detailing is largely transactional. Customer needs a detail, finds a detailer, gets it done, maybe comes back in 6 months. The relationship is casual.

Aircraft detailing is a maintenance relationship. The same G650 needs a wash every 30 days, wax every 90 days, and a ceramic inspection every 6 months. The flight department expects you to track these intervals and proactively schedule service — not wait for them to call.

This means your CRM needs:

  • Per-aircraft service interval tracking
  • Automated reminders when service is due
  • Job history per tail number (not just per customer)
  • Before/after photo documentation for each service
  • A customer portal where the fleet manager can see all aircraft status at a glance

Automotive apps don't track by VIN with service interval reminders. They don't need to. But we do.

The Stakes Are Different

A mistake on a $30,000 car is bad. A mistake on a $30 million aircraft is a career-ending lawsuit. The documentation requirements alone — what products were used, what condition the paint was in before and after, what areas were worked on — require a level of record-keeping that automotive apps aren't designed for.

Purpose-built aircraft detailing software includes before/after photo uploads per service area, product tracking per job, and condition documentation that serves as both a quality record and legal protection.

The Payment Structure Is Different

Automotive detail shops typically take payment at the counter. Tap, swipe, done. Aircraft detailing involves deposits, progress payments, net-30 invoicing for fleet accounts, and platform fees that need to be transparent to both parties.

Your software needs to handle deposits that reserve scheduling, balance-due invoicing after completion, and the ability for customers to pay online through the quote link — not just a card reader at the shop.

So What Should You Use?
