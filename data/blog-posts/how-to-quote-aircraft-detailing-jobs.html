How to Quote Aircraft Detailing Jobs: A Step-by-Step Guide

The number one question I get from detailers breaking into aviation is: "How do I price this?" It's a fair question. The first time you stand under a Global 7500 and try to figure out what to charge, it's intimidating. The aircraft is 111 feet long, the paint is worth more than most houses, and the owner expects perfection.

After 15 years and thousands of quotes, here's how I approach it.

Step 1: Know Your Numbers

Everything starts with labor hours. Not guesses — actual calculated hours based on aircraft surface area and the specific service being performed. A one-step polish on a Citation CJ3 (roughly 1,950 sq ft of surface) is a fundamentally different job than a one-step polish on a Gulfstream G550 (roughly 6,000 sq ft).

Here are rough ranges to calibrate your thinking:

Aircraft Wash Polish Ceramic
Citation CJ3 8h 35h 44h
King Air 350 8h 34h 43h
Gulfstream G4 18h 76h 101h
Global 7500 33h 148h 185h

These are single-person hours. If you're running a crew of two, divide by two for calendar time (but the total labor hours — and therefore the cost — stays the same).

Step 2: Set Your Labor Rate

Your hourly rate needs to cover more than just your time. It needs to cover:

  • Your labor
  • Product cost (typically 5-15% of the job)
  • Equipment wear and depreciation
  • Insurance
  • Travel time and fuel
  • Business overhead (software, phone, marketing)
  • Profit margin

Most experienced aircraft detailers charge between $125-$250/hour depending on market, experience, and specialization. If you're doing ceramic coatings and paint correction, you should be at the higher end. If you're primarily doing maintenance washes, the volume makes up for a lower rate.

Don't set your rate based on what automotive detailers charge. Different industry, different risk profile, different client expectations.

Step 3: Factor in Access Difficulty

Not all jobs are created equal. A King Air parked in a clean hangar with power and water is a standard job. The same King Air on a remote ramp in July with no shade and no water hookup is a 15-25% harder job. Your quote should reflect this.

I use a simple multiplier:

  • Standard (hangar, utilities available): 1.0x
  • Moderate (ramp with some shade, water available): 1.1x
  • Difficult (open ramp, limited utilities): 1.15x
  • Very Difficult (remote, no utilities, extreme weather): 1.25x

Step 4: Build the Quote Professionally

Your quote is often the first impression the aircraft owner has of your business. A text message that says "G4 detail $15k" tells the owner nothing and inspires zero confidence.

A professional quote should include:

  • Aircraft make, model, and tail number
  • Itemized services with individual pricing
  • Estimated duration
  • Proposed date
  • Payment terms
  • What's included and what's not

The fastest way to do this is with software that has the aircraft hours pre-loaded. Select the aircraft, check the services, adjust if needed, and send. The customer gets a clean, branded quote page — not a PDF buried in their inbox.

Step 5: Present Options, Not Just a Number

One of the most effective pricing strategies I've learned is to present two or three options. Instead of one quote for $18,000, present:

  • Maintenance Wash + Interior: $3,200
  • Full Detail with One-Step Polish: $12,500
  • Full Detail with Ceramic Coating: $28,000

The customer self-selects into the tier that matches their budget and expectations. You close more often because there's always an option that works. And the premium option anchors the mid-tier as reasonable by comparison.

Step 6: Follow Up

If you send a quote and wait for the phone to ring, you'll wait a long time. Follow up within 48 hours. Not with a "just checking in" email — with a specific question: "Would you like to schedule for next Tuesday? I have availability at KTEB."

The best CRMs track when the customer opens your quote and prompt you to follow up at the right moment.

The Bottom Line

Quoting aircraft detailing jobs isn't guesswork — it's math. Hours × rate × access difficulty = price. Know your hours, know your rate, present it professionally, and follow up. Do that consistently and you'll close more work at better margins than the detailer who's still texting prices from memory.
