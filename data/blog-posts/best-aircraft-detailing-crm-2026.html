The Best Aircraft Detailing CRM in 2026: What to Look For

If you're searching for a CRM for your aircraft detailing business, you've probably already discovered the problem — there aren't many options built for our industry. Most search results point you to automotive detailing software, general field service tools, or enterprise CRMs that cost more per month than some of your jobs.

I've tested most of them over 15 years in this business. Here's what I've learned about what actually matters, and what's just marketing noise.

The Non-Negotiables

1. Aircraft-Specific Pricing Database

This is the single most important feature. If the CRM doesn't have a database of aircraft models with labor hour estimates per service, you're still doing math in your head or on a spreadsheet. The best systems have 200-300+ aircraft models with hours for each service type — exterior wash, polish, ceramic coating, interior detail, brightwork, carpet extraction.

When you select a Gulfstream G650 and check "One-Step Polish," the system should know that's approximately 110 hours of labor. Not because someone guessed — because the surface area has been calculated and the hours have been validated by actual detailers doing actual work.

2. Online Payment Collection

If your CRM can't collect payment through the quote, you're adding unnecessary steps. The best flow is: customer receives quote link → reviews services → accepts → pays → done. All in one session, on their phone, at the airport. Every extra step between "I want this" and "here's my card" loses you conversions.

3. Customer Portal with Photo History

Aircraft owners — especially fleet managers — want documentation. Before and after photos per job, organized by aircraft, accessible anytime. A CRM that lets you upload job photos and automatically makes them available in the customer's portal is worth its weight in gold. It builds trust, supports warranty claims on ceramic coatings, and makes rebooking effortless.

4. Recurring Service Tracking

The most profitable detailing operations aren't chasing new customers every month — they're maintaining relationships with existing customers on predictable intervals. Wax every 90 days. Maintenance wash monthly. Ceramic inspection every 6 months. Your CRM should track these intervals per aircraft and alert you (and the customer) when service is approaching.

Nice-to-Haves That Actually Matter

FAA Tail Number Lookup

When a customer gives you their N-number, the system should pull the aircraft make, model, and year from the FAA registry automatically. Saves time and eliminates errors from customers who tell you they have a "Gulfstream" when they actually have a Bombardier.

Google Calendar Integration

If you're scheduling jobs, the CRM should check your calendar availability and suggest open slots. Manual calendar management is a recipe for double-booking, especially during busy season.

Quote Request Widget

A widget or form you can embed on your website that walks the customer through a quote request — aircraft type, services needed, airport location — and delivers the request directly to your CRM. No more back-and-forth emails just to gather basic information.

Mobile-First Design

You're not sitting at a desk. You're at the airport, in the hangar, on the ramp. Your CRM needs to work perfectly on your phone — creating quotes, viewing customer history, uploading photos, checking your schedule. If the mobile experience is an afterthought, the whole tool is an afterthought.

What I'd Skip

AI that writes your quotes for you. Your customers are paying for your expertise, not a chatbot's. AI can help with data lookup and calculations, but the final quote should reflect your professional judgment.

Inventory management. Unless you're running a shop with significant product inventory, this adds complexity without value. Most mobile detailers don't need it.

Complex team management for solo operators. If it's just you, don't pay for a tool designed for a 20-person operation. Scale up when you need to.

The Cost Question

Most aircraft detailing CRMs fall in the $79-$149/month range for a single operator. That sounds like money until you realize that one accurate quote — built in 3 minutes instead of 45 — pays for the software for the month. If the tool saves you from underquoting even one large job per quarter, the ROI is 10x or more.

Free tiers exist and they're worth trying. You should be able to build at least a few quotes before deciding whether the tool fits your workflow.
